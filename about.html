Quana's Story From Struggle to Sanctuary

Every rug I create is woven with the threads of my grandmother's wisdom, my mother's strength, and my own dreams.

I grew up in a small apartment in South Chicago, where my grandmother would sit by the window every evening, her weathered hands moving rhythmically through colorful fabrics. She was making quiltsâ€”not just any quilts, but masterpieces that told stories of our ancestors, of struggles overcome, and of hope that refused to die.

I was eight years old when she first placed a needle in my hand. "Baby girl," she said with that warm smile that could light up the darkest room, "your hands were made to create beauty. Don't you ever forget that." At the time, I didn't fully understand what she meant. I was just a little Black girl from the South Side, dreaming beyond what seemed possible.

Life wasn't easy. My mother worked three jobs to keep us afloat. There were nights when dinner was thin, and winters when the heat barely worked. But our home? Our home was always beautiful. My grandmother made sure of that. Her quilts draped every surface, bringing warmth not just to our bodies, but to our souls.

But I remembered my grandmother's words: "Your hands were made to create beauty." So I kept going. One rug became two. Two became ten. Ten became a collection. And suddenly, people weren't just buying rugsâ€”they were buying pieces of my soul, woven with every struggle, every triumph, every tear, and every smile.

Today, Rugs by Quana isn't just a business. It's a testament to every Black woman who's been told she can't. It's a love letter to my grandmother's legacy. It's proof that beauty can emerge from hardship, and that our hands were indeed made to create.

When you bring one of my rugs into your home, you're not just getting floor covering. You're getting a piece of history, a story of resilience, and a reminder that beauty and strength can coexist in the same space.
